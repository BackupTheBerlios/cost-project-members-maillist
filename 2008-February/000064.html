This is my reply sent to Henrik yesterday:

You want an estimate of landings-at-length, and you have samples 
randomly taken from some sampling units (probably trips) for which you 
also have a measure of effort. The question is how to combine the 
samples, and in particular whether you should weight them by the effort.

The correct answer is that it depends on how dependent on the trip size 
the proportion at length is. There are basically two possible 
estimators: One is the mean of the sample proportions, the other is the 
weighted mean. The weighted mean always has a higher variance than the 
unweighted mean, but it is unbiased, so which is the best depends on how 
strong the dependence is (this will increase the bias of the unweighted 
estimator), and how different the weights are (this will increase the 
variance of the weighted estimator). If you have  enough samples you 
could do some bootstrapping to test this.

This is exactly the same problem we get when raising age samples to get 
landings-at-age. Different countries have different approaches. We have 
done some analysis of the problem for the Norwegian catch, and have 
found no effect of trip size, and so we use something similar to the 
unweighted estimate (we have a modelling approach, which is more 
complicated but allows us to do many things such as estimate missing 
strata, include sample size differences etc). I think the reason for 
this is that the stratification takes out most of the differences both 
in trip size and in proportions at length, so what is left is mostly 
random rather than systematic. The only exception we have found is in 
small coastal fisheries where the metier stratification doesn't really 
describe the differences in fishing patterns within the strata.
