>>>> There are two points here. The first is that the only genuinely unbiased 
>>>> estimator (at least for proportion of landings at length) would be the 
>>>> one weighted by the number (not the weight) of landings (not catch) in 
>>>> each trip. Using weight of landings in each trip would certainly not be 
>>>> unbiased, because bigger fish weigh more, and so you would be 
>>>> overestimating the number of large fish. It's difficult to say what the 
>>>> effect of using effort would be.
>>>>
>>>> The second point is that the variance of the estimator is just as 
>>>> important as the bias. What you want is an estimate of the total that is 
>>>> as close to the true value as you can get. If the variance of your 
>>>> estimator is high, you will be no closer than if the bias is high. The 
>>>> only difference with bias is that in the long run, if you make enough 
>>>> estimates, an unbiased estimator will give you as many estimates that 
>>>> are too big as it will give estimates that are too small (roughly 
>>>> speaking). This is of no real value because you are interested in the 
>>>> individual estimate, not some long term properties of all your estimates 
>>>> combined. A good criterion to choose an estimate is the mean squared 
>>>> error, which is what it sounds like, the difference between the truth 
>>>> and your estimate, squared. This is equal to the bias squared plus the 
>>>> variance.
>>>>
>>>> The extra variance of the weighted estimator can be substantial, 
>>>> certainly it can be greater than the squared bias of the unweighted 
>>>> estimator. It just depends on the process behind the way fish are caught.

>>> Dear all,
>>>
>>> the general approach to raising, _to be discussed and agreed between 
>>> all_, should be the following
>>>
>>> *Raising to the total landings (length distribution)*
>>>
>>> Data used to raise are (i) total volume of landings of one species or 
>>> (ii) total volume of a commercial category of one species
>>> (i) the sampling unit is a trip and within each stratum, the samples 
>>> are raised to the trips before being summed and raised to the stratum
>>> (ii) the sampling unit is a commercial category and within each 
>>> stratum, the samples are simply summed and raised to the stratum.
>>> The rationale is to raise to the sampling unit first before summing 
>>> the samples: (i) is the trip so we raise, (ii) is the CC so we don't 
>>> need to raise.
>>>
>>> *Raising to the total catches (total number or total weight or length 
>>> distribution of retained part and discarded part)*
>>>
>>> Data used to raise are the (i) total number of sampled units (Fishing 
>>> operations, trips), (ii) total effort (number of days or hours), (iii) 
>>> any auxiliary variable based on the total landings (species analysed, 
>>> target species, all species, ...)
>>>
>>> If the retained part is sorted by CC and we know the total weight by 
>>> CC at the trip level and/or at the population level (total landings 
>>> per CC), we don't need to raise. The sampling unit is here the CC. In 
>>> all other cases (where the sampling unit is the FO), there is the 
>>> choice to raise to the trip and then to the population using the 
>>> number of fishing operations, the effort or an auxiliary variable, 
>>> based on the best correlations and accuracy of the variables used for 
>>> raising as suggested by WKDRP (San Sebastian, Feeb 2007)
>>> The EDA package will provide the necessary exploratory graphs and the 
>>> raising methods should propose all the possibilities. We have all the 
>>> formulations available and the user will have to make his mind on 
>>> which is the best method to chose.
>>>
>>> *Raising the age, weight, maturity and sex*
>>>
>>> I launch the discussion by proposing that this process comes to 
>>> creating SMWALKs (Sex-Maturity-Weight-Age-Length-Keys), i.e. 
>>> proportion of SMWA-at-length and combine this matrix with the length 
>>> structure of the population. This results in a matrix raised at the 
>>> population level, where the marginal sums give you either the 
>>> SMWA-at-length or the SMWL-at-age, the columns give the 
>>> SMW-at-length/age and the lines give the SMW-at-age/length.
>>> The sampling unit is one fish and the strata are length + temporal + 
>>> geographical
>>> This discussion is a key issue to be further amended/approved by WKMOG 
>>> (how to derive maturity ogive) chaired by David Maxwell in June
>>>
>>> We should first agree on the raising methods before discussing the 
>>> statistical formulation of the variances (second step).

The fundamental issues here are how do we estimate landings and discards 
at age (or weight etc, and maybe you don't need discards at age ...) and 
most importantly how do we know the uncertainty in our estimates? The 
key point is that the age (and size) distribution of fish differ from 
haul to haul and trip to trip. This makes it extremely important to know 
what your sampling unit is. The sampling unit is the thing you will 
randomly sample your fish from.

First, raising landings from market sampling sorted into CCs: Joel says  
"The raising procedure is then simply to sum up all your samples by CC 
and raise them to the total landings of that CC."

Ok, that will give you a point estimate. The trouble is you don't have a 
random sample from the market, either within each CC or not. What you 
usually have is a combined sample from a few trips, each of which might 
be regarded as being randomly sampled. If you knew the trip information 
(eg if you had done your sampling directly from the trips), then you 
would get some estimate, probably by raising to the trip level (though 
this is not necessarily best). This estimate would have some uncertainty 
that you have a chance of measuring. When you follow Joel's advice, you 
get a different estimate (for a start there is no raising to the trip) 
even though your data is actually the same. So how are you going to 
measure the uncertainty here? It is very difficult because you have lost 
the trip information. It is no good just assuming every fish is 
independent, because this will grossly underestimate the uncertainty.

Another important point is in the estimation of the ALK. I show that 
p(a|l) cannot be constant unless p(a), p(l) and p(l|a) are constant, 
then Joel says "I agree, but p(a | l) constant does not mean that each 
of the three probabilities p(l | a), p(a) and p(l) are constants." So I 
don't know what Joel is agreeing with. My point is that the trip (or 
haul) must be the sampling unit for the ALK as well as for the length 
distribution. This has nothing to do with stratification. This means 
that it is no more correct to assume all the fish are randomly sampled 
for the ALK than for the length distribution. This means it is difficult 
to correctly estimate the uncertainty in the catch-at-age if the source 
of the ALK data is not known.

The final point is that Joel does not discuss the use of weighted or 
unweighted estimators (which is where this started). There is a comment 
in the ICES WKDRP that the group thought that unbiased estimators were 
best, regardless of the extra uncertainty. There is however no 
justification for this (and it is clearly not true, presented with an 
unbiased estimator with a variance of 10000, and another with a bias of 
1 but a variance of only 10 I think everyone would go for the biased 
estimator). This is a very important issue.

I still don't understand Joel's comment about proportion at length. All 
the estimation methods involve estimating the proportion at length in 
some unit (trip or haul or whatever) then using this in some way to 
estimate the proportion at length in the total (along with the total 
numbers).
