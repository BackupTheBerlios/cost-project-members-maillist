Hi all,
I fully agree with Joel in his comments about size categories. The categoriesl is simply a sampling stratification level and it is as Joel writes important that the size categories are consistant in both Commercial Sampling data and Commercial Landing data at least for each  country. Originally, the categories was defined as EU size categories, which is well defined as weight ranges (individually for each species). The big advance is that this allows you to borrow sampling data from one country to another (or from one stratum to another) when no biological information is available for a given stratum of landings and it makes it much more manageble to coordinate sampling for a given area across crountries. If a given country have its own definitions on size categories (still consistant in both CS and CL records) the raising by weight will work but it is not possible to extrapolate CL data to another country. The definition of the size categories used is indicated in variable number 11 ("Size category scale") in the CL exchange format and variable number 12 in species list in CS exchange format. 
If the size category is diferent from trip to trip (or vessel to vessel) and therefore incompatible with the aggregated landing statistics (CL) for a given metier, it has no meaning as a sampling stratification and the data shall be considered as "unsorted".

Concerning the remark for the sub-sample weight at the marked sampling, this was done (in FishFrame) because we always regards marked samples as a subsample of all the landings at the marked (It was very often not possible to refer the sample to a given trip). As we don't want to have individual exchange formats for marked sampling compared with e.g. Sea-sampling, we just put in the sample weight as a dummy for the catch weight.

Dear all,

per definition, size category sampling means that the characteristics of the sorting is similar for all the trips, and the sampling unit is a size category. Of course, the degree of similarity varies from one trip to another, etc... but the most important is that the size grades seen on board (CS tables) can be raised to the overall weight of the same size grades estimated from the landings statistics (CL table).

When it is not the case (as is the Scottish case), the sampling strategy becomes a métier sampling, i.e. the sampling unit is a Fishing Operation or a Fishing trip. The size category is a mean to organise the sub-sampling and help the sampler to be more precise and accurate. 
In this case, the information estimated from the landings statistics is the weight landed for that métier (combination of gear, target species, area and time). The information in HL is the number of fish really measured, corresponding to SubSample weight in SL. The raising factor is SL$Weight/SL$Subsample weight.

Concerning the comment "For market sampling: Catch weight is per definition equal to Sample weight.". I understand it's confusing, as there is the possibility to subsample in the case of métier sampling. 
Maybe Teunis could give us his thoughts about this. I would think it's just a problem of label.

In all cases, summing over the HL$No at length should always give us the number of fish sampled.

I also reiterate my demand to get more datasets available from ALL the institutes involved in COST. this is becoming URGENT and must be given highest priority.
